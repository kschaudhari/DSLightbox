BAY OF ISLANDS, NEW ZELAND

Set off in search of dolphins, whales and the eye-popping Hole in the Rock around the Bay of Islands. Move to the end of the Cape Brett Peninsula, where you can gawp admiringly at the famous Hole in the Rock on Motu Kōkako Island. Māori warriors used to paddle through the Hole in the Rock in their canoes before setting off for battle; if drops of water from the cave roof above fell on them, it was a good omen for the fight ahead. If the weather allows it, why don’t navigate the Dolphin Seeker through the narrow space (if drops fall on your head, your enemies are done for)?

SPITTAL POND NATURE RESERVE, BERMUDA

Gawp at wildlife at Spittal Pond Nature Reserve, the largest and the most premium nature reserve and national park of the island. Look out for wildflowers, white long tails and even whales, perfect your swing on one of the island’s famous golf courses, hike up Fort Scaur (Bermuda’s highest hilltop) and explore the stalactite-stuffed crystal caves near Castle Harbour. Walk or cycle over the Bermuda Railway Trail, which spans from one end of the island to the other, with the majority of the 18-mile track traversing Bermuda’s scenic northern shore. (You’ll have earned at least three rum swizzles each by the time you’ve ticked off the track’s entirety).

HEAD OF BIGHT, SOUTH AUSTRALIA

For Cetecea lovers, South Australia, and in particular Head of Bight, 78kms west of Yalata on the Eyre Peninsula, is widely regarded as one of the best places in the world to experience the awesome spectacle of migrating whales. Part of the Great Australian Bight Marine Park, Australia’s second largest marine park after the Great Barrier Reef, this is where the white sand dunes and beaches meet the Bunda cliffs of the Nullarbor.

The most reliable time of year to view these wonderful creatures in their natural habitat is in July and August. Having left the icy Antarctic waters in May for the warmer coastal comfort of their South Australian breeding grounds, up to 100 whales, including calves, can be seen in the waters adjacent to the Bunda Cliffs until early October.

GREECES’S BEACHES ARE PRETTIER THAN ADONIS

Join the locals topping up golden tans at sandy Psarou Beach, which is so popular with the holidaying jet-set, there’s a waiting list for sun loungers in August. If you like your beaches red and rugged, you’ll be a big fan of Red Beach, whose vividly hued sand earns the beach its name. To get to the water, you’ll need to clamber over a rocky, hilly path for about 10 minutes, meaning you’ll have more than earned your cooling dip.

Want to sunbathe on a beach shaped liked the Greek letter omega? Look no further than Voidokilia, whose sand dunes form a semicircular strip, guarded by the Old Frankish castle (Palaiokastro) and the cave of King Nestor.

SPOILED FOR CHOICE, KENYA

With a number of different coastal areas to choose from there is something for every beach lover in Kenya. From the historical town of Mombasa and the bustling resorts on the southern coast to the Northern beaches of Malindi and Watamu, which offer unparalleled scuba diving, kite surfing and deep sea fishing - the coast is the perfect end to any break in Kenya.

The coast is home to a world beating range of marine species such starfish, cucumber sea urchins and turtles. The migratory birds are also a sight to behold.
