welcome to
KAUAI

aloha

Come to Kauai for peace and quiet, immersive nature, serene rivers, relaxation, stunning scenery, rejuvenation and a sense of Hawaii’s history.

close up

Welcome to Kauai (hint, it’s pronounced ‘kuh-WUH-ee’), Hawaii’s oldest island. The Garden Isle earns its name with tropical rainforests, tranquil valleys, jagged mountain peaks and hurtling waterfalls. Natural thrills abound here: more than 80 per cent of the island is inaccessible by car; Kauai has more beach per coastline than its neighbours and it is also home to Hawaii’s only navigable rivers.

VIEWFINDER

Expect blissfully uninterrupted views: no building on Kauai is taller than a coconut tree (that’s four-stories high, in case you’re not au fait with the average height of coconut trees). Follow Kauai’s one-lane highways and discover hidden waterfalls and secluded beaches.

FLASH

Napali Coast is one of the world’s most photogenic – and photographed – landscapes. It’s on the far west of the island, graced with craggy cliffs, postcard-perfect bays and beaches, plus mysterious caves, which can only be reached via boat or hiking trips. For a different perspective, take a helicopter tour over the coast with your camera at the ready.

WATERMARK

Kauai doesn’t hold back when it comes to beaches: Poipu Beach is famed for its calm, crystalline waters and crowd-free sands. Try surfing or paddleboarding (Poipu is equally good if you just want to lie down on sun-warmed sand). Can’t pick between mountains, beach and waterfalls? You don’t have to at Hanalei Beach Park, a two-kilometre-long crescent of milk-white sand, overlooked by waterfall-lined mountains.

ZOOM

There’s plenty to please modern-day adventurers: Waimea Canyon, nicknamed the Grand Canyon of the Pacific, offers incredible views and an abundance of hiking trails; cyclists can join a tour that includes a bike ride down Waimea Canyon Drive. Explore the island on horseback, or try river-kayaking, zip-lining and ATV tours. Don’t miss mountain-tubing: you’ll float down historic sugar plantation irrigation canals, through hand-dug tunnels, taking in the eye-widening scenery as you go.

MICRO LENS

Anaina Hou Community Park hosts a Farmers’ Market twice a week; it’s also home to a mini-golf course, cosy café and the five-mile Wai Koa Loop, a hiking trail whose lures include a mahogany forest, quiet lagoons and a hidden swimming hole. Pay a trip to one of the island’s lively fish markets, where you can linger over a just-caught takeaway lunch of succulent seafood, fresh fish, Hawaiian plates and daily specials, or pick up toothsome picnic provisions. Yogis will like Princeville Makai Golf Club, which doubles-up as an alfresco setting for sunrise yoga classes (while you’re here, join the popular Sunset Golf Cart tour).

welcome to
oahu

aloha

Oahu offers surf adventures, art and culture, history and heritage, world-famous beaches, top-notch dining and a vibrant, energised pace of life.

close up

It’s no mystery why Oahu’s nickname is ‘the Gathering Place’ – it’s home to two-thirds of Hawaii’s population and is the most-visited of the islands. The state capital, Honolulu, sits on the southeast coast; The South shore is rich in visitor attractions, including exhibits, tours and the world-famous Waikiki Beach. West of Honolulu is Pearl Harbor, which was famously devastated by the Japanese forces in 1941. The lagoon harbour is rich in visitor attractions, including memorials, battleships, submarines and an aviation museum.

VIEWFINDER

Leahi (Diamond Head) is a strong contender for Hawaii’s most distinctive landmark. The classic summit looms over Waikiki’s beaches like a giant dorsal fin (‘Le' Ahi’ means ‘brow’ or ‘back’). It’s worth hiking along the summit for incredible views of Oahu’s south and eastern shores.

FLASH

Don’t miss Windward Coast, on the island’s eastern side and home to hidden coves, beautiful beaches and rugged coastlines. In Honolulu, visit Chinatown for a sensory overload and pay a trip to ornate Lolani Palace, where Hawaii’s royal rulers once resided.

WATERMARK

When it comes to sun, sea and sand, Oahu’s headline act is Waikiki Beach, which should technically be called Waikiki Beaches: eight distinct sections make up the iconic two-mile stretch of sand. Stake out spots on less-crowded Fort DeRussy Beach Park, peaceful Queen Kapiolani Beach or San Souci Beach, famed for its snorkelling. Oahu’s North Shore hosts some of the world’s biggest surfing competitions in winter, including the Vans Triple Crown of Surfing (November–December). Waimea Bay was a pivotal surf spot in the Fifties; beautiful, curved Hanauma Bay is formed within a volcanic cone and home to a pristine marine ecosystem; Hawaii’s best snorkelling is rumoured to be had here.

FOCUS

Pearl Harbor delivers an interactive lesson on American and Pacific history via four key attractions: the USS Arizona Memorial, the USS Battleship Missouri, the Pacific Aviation Museum and the USS Bowfin Museum. Get a different history lesson at the Polynesian Cultural Center, which champions seven Polynesian cultures via replica villages.

MICRO LENS

Come to Oahu hungry and thirsty: the island’s culinary highlights include the fingerlicking-tasty, frill-free shrimp trucks parked up at Kahuku and Haleiwa (garlic-butter a-go-go), Portuguese-influenced treats at Leonard’s Bakery (binge on malasadas: hole-free doughnuts) and Rainbow Drive-In, rumoured to serve the best plate lunches on Oahu. Try a teriyaki beef plate or the loco moco: a bowl filled with rice, and layered with a hamburger patty, a fried egg and gravy. Have a sweet tooth? Get addicted to Hawaii’s famous shave-ice puddings: Waiola Shave Ice in Moiliili or Matsumoto’s in Haleiwa both dish up delicious versions of the snow-cone sugar-hit. Flex your plastic at Waikele Premium Outlet, 30 minutes from Honolulu, where retail-addicts can find big-name designer threads for pleasingly small prices.

welcome to
molokai

aloha

Welcome to the ‘Friendly Island’, where the aloha-spirit is alive and kicking, and things haven’t changed much in the last 50-odd years.

close up

Life moves at a deliciously slow pace here – there are no shopping centres, no lifts, no neon, no burger joints – and that’s just how the locals like it. (We reckon you will, too.) Molokai was once part of Maui Nui: a prehistoric volcanic island that later split into separate islands (inhabited Maui, Lanai and Molokai; uninhabited Kahoolawe and Molokini). Each island has its own distinct character: on rural, peaceful Molokai, you can live like a local and get a true flavour of Hawaiian culture, history and hospitality.

VIEWFINDER

Dust off your camera for the tranquil Kamakou Preserve, a lush 3,000-acre refuge that’s home to a dazzling array of around 250 rare plants, many of which can only be found in Hawaii. This magnificent rainforest is located high in the mountains of East Molokai, home to native songbirds, gem-bright insects and colourful banded tree snails. Follow the boardwalk that cuts through a moss-covered rain forest and pristine mountain bog, leading to a spectacular viewing area that spies on panoramic Pelekunu Valley.

FLASH

The eye-popping Kalaupapa Peninsula is a contender for ‘Most Beautiful Place in Hawaii’ (which is pretty impressive, given the strength of the competition).

ZOOM

Visit Kapuaiwa Coconut Beach Park, an ancient Hawaiian coconut grove planted in the 1860s during the reign of King Kamehameha. Pose for pictures by the colourful blooms that flourish at Molokai Plumeria Orchard, visit the island’s war memorial at downtown Kaunakakai and look out for humpback whales between December and mid April (the south side of the island and the beaches on the west end offer the best views).

WATERMARK

Molokai also has stunning beach attractions: Papohaku Beach (nicknamed Three Mile Beach, for obvious reasons) and One Alii Beach Park, which is named after an ancient Hawaiian fishpond. This spacious, untouched beach, four miles east of downtown Kaunakakai, is ripe for sun-worshippers...

MICRO LENS

Don’t miss Halawa Valley and Bay at the eastern end of the island, where you’ll discover historic landmarks, the state's longest pier, and enticing shops, cafés and restaurants. One of Molokai’s tastiest treats is hot bread slathered with a choice of toppings; hit the famous Kanemitsu Bakery around 10pm every night (except Monday) and join the locals queuing for their just-baked loaves, spread with melted butter, cinnamon, sugar, blueberry or strawberry jam, cream cheese – or all of the above.

welcome to
maui

aloha

Have a Hawaiian romance in mellow Maui, whose charms include small-town adventures, stirring sunsets, artisanal food thrills and sophisticated resort life.

close up

Charming Maui was once part of Maui Nui: a prehistoric volcanic island that split into separate islands (inhabited Maui, Lanai and Molokai; uninhabited Kahoolawe and Molokini). Each island has its own distinct character – Maui is spontaneous and adventurous, relaxed, fun and active. Find out why locals often say ‘Maui no ka oi’ – meaning ‘Maui is the best’...

VIEWFINDER

Begin your day in style on lofty Haleakala summit, where the epic sunrise is hard to beat.

WATERMARK

Windsurfers, rejoice: Maui’s Hookipa Beach is one of the world’s best spots for the sport. Maui’s other highlights for beach-lovers include Kaanapali’s blissful stretch of bleached-white sand, Makena Beach State Park (aka Big Beach) on Maui’s southwestern shores, and buzzing Wailea Beach, which has an up-tempo resort vibe. There’s also golden-crescent Kapalua, sheltered by volcanic-rock promontories.

FLASH

Do go chasing waterfalls in Maui, where the headline hurtling falls include mighty Wailua, just beyond Hana. Haleakala National Park is home to Waimoku Falls, a 400-foot giant that can be reached via the two-mile Pipiwai Trail.

ZOOM

Head for remote Hana, which is considered to be one of the last unspoiled Hawaiian frontiers. The 54-mile drive from Kahului is incredibly scenic, punctuated by hair-pin bends, narrow one-lane bridges, stretches of lush vegetation and cascading waterfalls. Nature-lovers are spoiled for choice here: go whale-watching in Lahaina, on the west side of the island, admire the United States’ largest banyan tree at the Banyan Tree Park, or go hiking in rugged Haleakala National Park (keep your eyes peeled for endangered Hawaiian geese). The park’s coastal region of Kipahulu is home to the Pools of Oheo: freshwater pools and waterfalls set in a tranquil bamboo forest.

MICRO LENS

Hale Kahiko is an impressive, 10,000 square-metre replica of an ancient Hawaiian village, complete with three striking hales (thatched-roof houses) and an extensive collection of ancient furnishings. Alternatively, explore bucolic Upcountry Maui and its friendly farms, where you can meet artisan producers and learn about Maui’s diverse, exciting food scene. Continue the relaxed pace of life by lounging poolside – with a refreshing tropical drink close by, natch – at one of Maui’s upscale resorts, from Kapalua to Wailea.

welcome to
lanai

aloha

Get away from it all on secluded, luxurious Lanai, which attracts green turtles, humpback whales and modern-day explorers.

close up

Don’t expect to see any traffic lights on Hawaii’s ‘most enticing island’, where only 30 miles of road are paved. Like Maui, Lanai was once part of Maui Nui: a prehistoric volcanic island. Each island has its own distinct character: Lanai offers solitude and serenity, natural highs and the choice of laid-back or fast-paced escapades. Lanai is affectionately nicknamed Pineapple Island – a nod to its fruity past as an island-wide pineapple plantation...

VIEWFINDER

You might think you’ve landed on Mars at Keahiakawelo (aka the Garden of the Gods), a striking, lunar landscape of otherworldly butts and spires; natural minerals in the soil have resulted in a vivid array of red, lavender, brown, ochre and purple hues. Take in pic-worthy vistas on the pine-lined Munro Trail (named after the 19th-century New Zealand naturalist, George Munro), which wends its way north of Lanai City, past the stables of the Four Seasons Resort Lanai. Follow the trail all the way to the top of Lanaihale (House of Lanai), the highest mountain in Lanai, measuring 3,370 feet.

FLASH

Admire sacred ruins at Kaunolu, an ancient fishing village overlooking the ocean, located at Lanai’s southern tip (you’ll need a 4WD to get here). King Kamehameha the Great had a soft spot for Kaunolu in the Seventies and it’s easy to see why – the village offers incredible views of Shark Fin Cove and Lanai’s southern sea cliffs, the remains of Halulu Heiau (a sacred temple), petroglyphs (rock carvings) and ‘Kahekili’s Leap’, where intrepid warriors used to display their bravery by diving off a 60-foot cliff.

ZOOM

Go hiking among native ohia lehua trees on the Munro Trail or look out for playful spinner dolphins gambolling near secluded Hulopoe Bay. Set off on a bottom-bouncing 4WD ride, uncovering Lanai’s off-the-beaten-track treasures. Enjoy a leisurely, sun-kissed picnic overlooking Puu Pehe (Sweetheart Rock), named after a legendary maiden called Puu and her handsome warrior. Channel Lanai’s romantic vibe and cuddle up to your own sweetheart.

WATERMARK

Pack towels and beachwear for the Four Seasons Resort Lanai at Manele Bay, or pay a trip to the island’s most accessible beach, Hulopoe, which offers brilliant swimming, snorkelling and marine-life viewing. Polihua (Lanai’s longest white-sand beach) or Kaiolohia (Shipwreck Beach) are also safe bets for sun-kissed seaside sessions.

MICRO LENS

Lanai looks after its visitors: there are two high-end Four Seasons Resorts on the island (pick from one by the sea, one in the mist-clad mountains); there’s also Hotel Lanai in Lanai City, which abounds with old-style plantation character and charm.

welcome to
island of hawaii

aloha

On Hawaii’s eponymous island, star-filled nights, diverse climates, dramatic volcanoes, inspiring nature and a lively, hospitable culture await.

close up

Getting confused between the Island of Hawaii and Hawaii state? Thankfully, the former has a nickname: the Big Island: a sensible moniker, given that it’s the largest of Hawaii’s six major islands. The island’s capital is Honolulu, worth a visit for its characterful downtown, home to wooden-fronted stores and a thriving arts-and-culture scene. Although the weather on Island of Hawaii is typically warm and temperate, it’s not a bad idea to bring something for cool nights and occasional rain – Hawaii has all but two of the world’s climate zones. Island of Hawaii is served by two airports: Hilo on the east coast; Kona on the west coast.

VIEWFINDER

Measuring in at nearly 14,000 feet from sea level, it’s no surprise that the dormant Mauna Kea volcano offers some of the world’s best stargazing. Don’t worry if you’re not a fan of dizzyingly high altitudes – some Island of Hawaii hotels offer seaside stargazing. You can also plug into the live feed from one of the observatories at Imiloa Astronomy Center.

FLASH

According to legend, you’ll share Island of Hawaii with Pele, the Hawaiian goddess of fire. Way back when, Pele shaped the Hawaiian Islands using molten lava; her legacy lives on today via five volcanoes: one extinct, one dormant and three active. Learn about them all at Hawaii Volcanoes National Park, where molten lava flows, expansive lava fields, prehistoric lava tubes, curling steam clouds, craggy calderas, heated steam vents and sulfur banks are all par for the course. The dramatically named Chain of Craters Road is as impressive as it sounds – expect apocalyptic landscapes, including Nahuku – aka Thurston Lava Tube – a 500-year-old lava cave hidden away in a tropical rainforest.

WATERMARK

Water-babies will be wooed by the Island of Hawaii’s colour-packed Kohala Coast and Kona beaches, which promise watersports, wave-side picnics, deep-sea fishing, scuba diving, sailing and more. Go snorkelling at Kahaluu Beach Park in Kona; pose for pictures on a black-sand beach (Punaluu in Kau is a popular choice); explore one of the multi-landscaped beach parks: Hapuna Beach State Park, Spencer Beach Park or Ohai Ula, perhaps...

ZOOM

Get your thrills at Akaka Falls State Park, where two of Hawaii’s most eye-watering waterfalls can be admired: Akaka is the state’s tallest sheer-drop waterfall, making Niagara Falls look weedy. Get a different kind of buzz at Holualoa, a little village in Kona coffee country, packed with interesting boutiques, art galleries, revamped plantation shacks, sociable cafés and more. Ever heard of Hawaiian cowboys? Meet the paniolo – Hawaii’s ranching community – in Waimea’s lush green pastures.

MICRO LENS

Island of Hawaii hosts the famous annual Merrie Monarch Hula Festival in Easter – but don’t worry if you can’t get tickets, island-hub Hilo hosts plenty of spin-off events.
