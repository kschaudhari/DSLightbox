OVERVIEW

There is nothing in sport quite like the Ashes, with its epic history – with its mythical mix of friendship and enmity between the two protagonists, England and Australia; 376 Test matches, over 69 series, across 140 years; its brilliant players; its unique trophy – the charred remains of a bail, housed in a terracotta urn; the alien weather and conditions for touring sides; its five, six or even seven-Test series.

England embark this winter on what, so often for them, has been their toughest tour. Since Ivo Bligh regained the ‘Ashes’ in 1882/83, English tourists have found the experience exciting and all-consuming in equal measure.
The Test matches in Perth and Adelaide have offered a similar mix. Beauty, joy, intensity, heartache. The Adelaide Oval, venue for this winter’s second Test from December 2-6, was a sublime, poetic cricket venue. With its grass viewing areas, old scoreboard and the Don Bradman Pavilion, there were fears that any modernisation would ruin the beauty. Those apprehensions were unfounded. “The way they have changed it has been absolutely magnificent,” says former England captain Nasser Hussain. “You would have thought they could not make it any better but they have.”

Weirs Cove, Kangaroo Island

The pink balls and floodlights in this winter’s Ashes Test will also provide novelty. Then the series moves to Perth, from December 14-18. The golden sunshine, the cracks in the pitch that any fast bowler craves, the sea breeze picking up, the ball flying through to the wicketkeeper, gloves pointing to the azure sky. Perth has a reputation for compelling Test cricket – and the five days there this time are expected to be no different. Ashes to Ashes. The legend continues.

ON THE FIELD

The golden sunshine, the cracks in the pitch that any fast bowler craves, the sea breeze, known locally as the Fremantle Doctor, the ball flying through to the wicketkeeper, gloves pointing to the azure sky – a Test match at Perth is like nowhere else on earth.

WACA, Perth

Mike Hussey was born and raised in Perth, and thrived at the WACA in Ashes Tests, scoring 74 and 103 in 2006/07, and 61 and 116 in 2010/11 – both of which led to crushing Australian victories over England. “It is a positive, attacking venue,” he says. “Spectators know they are going to see teams going for it because of the conditions, not the sort of turgid cricket we sometimes get in Tests. It’s also a good ‘seeing’ ground – the light is pure and clean.”

Adelaide Oval, Day-night test

Those entasked with updating the glorious Adelaide Oval faced an onerous assignment. Set amid parklands and gardens of fig trees, with its wonderful view of St Peter’s Cathedral and its exquisite, manually operated scoreboard, it was one of the most beautiful grounds in the world. The fact that it is stillone of the most picturesque proves that the architects were successful.

“Memories of Tests there in my era were certainly mixed,” admits Nasser Hussain, who was a player in 1998/99, and led England in 2002/03. “It was usually hot, we usually lost the toss, Australia usually got loads of runs!

Phil Tufnell has tasted some success in Australia – but not much on the cricket field! He won I’m A Celebrity… Get Me Out Of Here! in 2003 but was on the losing England side in the Ashes of 1990/91 and 1994/95 – although he did not lose either of the Tests at the Adelaide Oval.

OFF THE FIELD

Adelaide is preparing for an invasion of English cricket fans for the first ever day-night Ashes Test in December. It presents the perfect opportunity for these visitors to get out and explore the state of South Australia before, during and after the match itself.

The Lane Vineyard, Hahndorf

There can be no doubt with cricket lovers, that the legacy of Sir Donald Bradman resonates strongly at the Adelaide Oval. However, when we met his granddaughter, Greta, she is keen to stress there are many non-cricket reasons to visit South Australia.

For those looking to venture out of town, Greta recommends a drive up through the Adelaide Hills and south to McLaren Vale – part of a self-drive route known as the Epicurean Way.

Perth is a place like no other - Australia’s sunniest city, its urban pleasures sits between 19 city beaches plus the pristine bush of Kings Park – the largest inner city park in the world. Perth is famed for its wildlife, where you can spot wild dolphins in the morning and head to award winning wineries in the Swan Valley in the afternoon.

Kings Park, Perth

David Gower, the elegant former England batsman, first developed a soft spot for Western Australia as a young cricketer, when he spent four months living in Perth and travelling the length and breadth of the state. He played for Claremont Cottesloe CC in Perth grade cricket, and spent many happy days on Cottesloe Beach, with its “white sand and lovely warm Indian Ocean”.

World-class food and wine, stunning landscapes and unique native wildlife. It is little wonder Adelaide and South Australia have become high on the list for British holidaymakers heading Down Under.
