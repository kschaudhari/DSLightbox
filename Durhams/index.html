Durham adventures

There’s more to Durham than a UNESCO-listed cathedral and castle (as mind-boggling as they are). Landscapes with truly special status (the North Pennines Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty and Durham Heritage Coast), for example. It’s up to you how you experience Durham’s spectacular tapestry of woodlands, waterfalls, moorlands, forests, fields, beaches and more, but here are some ideas to get you started. (Now might be a good time to eat a pease pudding or two, just to get your energy levels up.)

Walking

Set your own pace and take in the county’s incredible views on two feet. In the Durham Dales, look out for feathered locals (merlin, black grouse, golden plover, curlew, ring ouzel, kingfishers, dippers, buzzards and more) on the Weardale Way, which follows the River Wear. If you prefer waterfalls to wildlife, tick off both Low and High Force on the Waterfalls Walk (refuel at the refurbished Bowlees Visitor Centre, conquering cake and tea). For majestic moorland views, stride past upland sheep farms and across country on the Waskerley Way, keeping your eyes peeled for rare, jewel-bright butterflies (especially between May and late autumn). Coastal walks don’t get much more jaw-dropping than the one that takes in Blackhall Rocks and Nose’s Point en route to the lively harbour town of Seaham; enjoy a stroll around the marina and a well-deserved ice-cream.

Water

There’s plenty for budding mermen and mermaids to do in Durham. Start by chasing waterfalls (safely) with Kingsway Adventure Centre, where a gorge walk will lead you to spectacular sights, such as Low Force. If you’re feeling brave enough, follow in Ben Fogle’s footsteps and try some waterfall jumping or white-water swimming for liquid thrills (don’t attempt either without expert supervision). Learn some piratical skills with the help of the Seaham Harbour Activity Centre, where you can go surfing, kayaking, canoeing, windsurfing, paddle-boarding and more. Discover a different side to Durham’s Heritage Coast…

Wheels

Durham’s varied landscapes are guaranteed to make cyclists’ hearts skip a beat. Criss-crossing the county, hundreds of miles of cycle paths, disused railway lines, quiet country lanes and off-road trails are waiting to be explored, with options for every age and ability. Let Hamsterley Trailblazers take you on a whirlwind tour of Hamsterley Forest, home to roughly 30 miles of loopy mountain bike trails. There are different trail-levels marked out amid the evergreens, from blue (beginner) to black (advanced). Hamsterley Trailblazers is a volunteer organisation with a passion for the forest and its bountiful bike thrills; spend an hour or so with one of their guides and you’ll soon see why. Don’t just keep your eyes on the track: the trails come with eye-popping views across the Durham Dales and the North Pennines Area of Outstanding Natural Beauty (a UNESCO European and Global Geopark). Thanks to the stellar efforts of Hamsterley Trailblazers, this forest is only going to get better and better: the group is on a mission to build an extra 2km of track every year. If you’d like to give your legs an easy ride, hire electric bikes and wheel around without tensing a tendon.
